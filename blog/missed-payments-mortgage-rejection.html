Missed Payments – Will Banks Reject Your Mortgage Application?

By Finch Mortgage | Updated April 2026 | Auckland, New Zealand

10 min read

You’ve saved a deposit, found a house you love, and you're ready to apply for a mortgage. But there’s a looming fear in the back of your mind: last year, you accidentally missed a credit card payment. Will the bank immediately reject your application? Will one simple oversight derail your dream of homeownership?

The short answer is no, a single missed payment won’t automatically result in a rejection, but it depends heavily on the context, the type of debt, and how recently it occurred.

Banks and lenders assess your 'account conduct' rigorously under New Zealand’s responsible lending laws. In this guide, we’ll dive into exactly how banks scrutinize your financial history, what kind of missed payments matter most, and how to mitigate the damage.

1. Why Banks Care About Account Conduct

Under the Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Act (CCCFA), lenders are legally required to ensure they are not putting you in a position of hardship by granting you a loan. To do this, they don't just look at how much you earn; they look at how you manage what you earn.

Your bank statements (usually the last 3 to 6 months) and your credit report are a window into your financial character. Lenders are looking for patterns of behavior. A missed payment suggests to a lender that you either lack the funds to meet your obligations (an affordability issue) or you lack the discipline to manage your obligations (a character issue).

2. Not All Missed Payments Are Created Equal

A bank credit assessor differentiates between different types of missed payments. Some are minor red flags, while others are full stop signs.

The Minor Offenses: Utilities and Subscriptions

Did you miss a Netflix payment because your debit card expired? Did a power bill bounce because you forgot to transfer money from your savings account to your checking account? While not ideal, banks generally overlook these if they are isolated incidents. If your statements show that you immediately rectified the missed payment within a day or two, a simple written explanation is usually sufficient.

The Moderate Offenses: Credit Cards and Buy-Now-Pay-Later

Missing a payment on a credit card, personal loan, or an Afterpay/Laybuy account is more serious. These are formal credit facilities. Comprehensive Credit Reporting (CCR) in New Zealand means that your payment history (whether you pay on time each month) is recorded on your credit file.

If you have a 30-day late payment recorded on your file, it will lower your credit score. If a bank sees a recent missed credit card payment, they will ask questions. Was it an oversight? Or are you struggling to make ends meet? One missed payment from two years ago is easily explained away. Two missed payments in the last three months will likely result in a decline.

The Severe Offenses: Existing Mortgages and Rent

If you are refinancing an existing mortgage or applying for a new one, banks will heavily scrutinize your current housing costs. Missing a mortgage payment or missing a rent payment is a cardinal sin in the eyes of a credit assessor. If you cannot reliably pay for the roof over your head now, the bank will not trust you with a half-million-dollar loan. A missed mortgage payment within the last 12 months makes it incredibly difficult to get approved by a main bank.

3. The Golden 90-Day Rule

If you have a history of messy account conduct—perhaps a few unarranged overdrafts or a bounced direct debit—the best thing you can do is hit pause on your house hunt.

Main banks typically require 90 days of spotless account conduct leading up to your application. This means 3 full months of bank statements showing:

  • Zero unarranged overdraft fees.
  • Zero dishonored or reversed payments.
  • Consistent, on-time payments of all bills and debts.
  • A clear demonstration that you are living within your means and saving money.

If you have a recent blip, waiting until you have 3 months of clean statements before applying is far better than applying immediately and getting declined.

4. How to Explain a Missed Payment

If a missed payment is visible on your bank statements or credit file, do not hope the bank will ignore it. They won't. The best approach is proactive transparency. This is where a mortgage broker is invaluable.

When Finch Mortgage submits an application on your behalf, we write a covering letter to the bank’s credit assessor. If there is a missed payment, we address it head-on with a logical, verifiable explanation.

Example of a Bad Explanation: "I just forgot to pay it." (Shows a lack of financial discipline).

Example of a Good Explanation: "The client missed their credit card minimum payment in October. As evidenced by the attached statements, they were overseas on holiday and had issues accessing their internet banking. The payment was made in full immediately upon their return. They have since set up an automated direct debit to ensure this does not happen again."

The key is to show the bank that the event was isolated, the root cause has been addressed, and the risk of it happening again is zero.

5. What If the Bank Says No?

If a main bank declines your application due to missed payments or poor account conduct, you still have options. As discussed in our Bad Credit Mortgage Guide, specialist non-bank lenders (like Resimac or Pepper Money) are far more forgiving of recent financial hiccups.

Specialist lenders understand that life happens. If a missed payment was due to a temporary redundancy or illness, and you are now back on your feet, they will often approve the loan. The trade-off is a slightly higher interest rate for a period of 1 to 2 years, after which you can refinance back to a main bank once your conduct is demonstrably clean.

Conclusion

A single missed payment is rarely a dealbreaker, provided it isn't part of a larger pattern of financial stress. The most crucial factor is how you manage your finances in the 3 to 6 months immediately preceding your mortgage application.
