How Much Deposit is Actually Needed for an NZ Home Loan?

By Finch Mortgage | Updated April 2026 | Auckland, New Zealand

8 min read April 2025

The phrase "How much deposit do I need?" is the most fiercely debated question in New Zealand real estate. With median house prices demanding exorbitant capital simply to pass the bank's front door, countless Kiwis assume homeownership is entirely out of reach. While the traditional 20% deposit rule remains the golden standard for securing the most preferential lending terms, the modern reality is that First Home Buyers have an arsenal of tools at their disposal to bypass this requirement entirely.

The Mechanics of the 20% Deposit Standard

To understand why the 20% threshold exists, we must look to the Reserve Bank of New Zealand (RBNZ). The RBNZ orchestrates the financial stability of the country and imposes Loan-to-Value Ratio (LVR) restrictions on retail banks. These macroeconomic 'speed limits' dictate that banks are only permitted to issue a very small percentage of their total residential lending to owner-occupiers who have less than a 20% deposit. By mandating a 20% equity buffer, the RBNZ ensures that if the housing market completely crashes and property values plummet by 15%, the bank is theoretically insulated from financial loss if forced to enact a mortgagee sale.

For a prospective buyer, hitting the 20% mark secures immediate advantages. First, you avoid entirely the Low Equity Margin (LEM)—an agonizing artificial inflation of your interest rate. Second, you circumvent the necessity of paying for a registered valuation prior to settlement, and your application bypasses the severest tiers of bank scrutiny. However, compiling $160,000 in cash to purchase a modest $800,000 starter home takes immense time. Thankfully, alternative pathways exist.

Entering the Market at 10% or 15% (Low Equity Lending)

If you have managed to scrape together 10% of the purchase price, you cross a critical psychological and financial threshold in the eyes of the bank. While standard LVR restrictions tightly constrain how many of these loans banks can issue, they absolutely still process them for extremely high-quality applicants.

To secure a 10% 'Low Equity' loan from a major main-street bank, your application must be bulletproof. You must exhibit impeccable Account Conduct (no unarranged overdraft fees, no missed payments), a stellar credit score, incredibly sturdy and unburdened cash flow, and steady employment history. Essentially, the bank compensates for your lack of capital by demanding perfection in your character and income.

The caveat is the Low Equity Margin (LEM). If you borrow 90% of a property's value, the bank views this as statistically riskier and penalizes you by adding an arbitrary premium to your interest rate—frequently between 0.50% and 1.50%. This can equate to hundreds of dollars a month in sheer dead money. The goal is to rapidly pay down the principal over the first few years (or wait for capital gains) until your equity breaches the 20% mark, at which point you actively request the bank strip the penalty away.

The 5% Deposit: Kāinga Ora's First Home Loan

For those feeling entirely locked out, the government (via Kāinga Ora) administers the First Home Loan. This scheme obliterates the 20% barrier by allowing eligible first home buyers to acquire a property with just a 5% deposit. Crucially, Kāinga Ora acts as the underwriter, effectively guaranteeing the loan for the retail bank. Because the bank carries zero risk, they willingly extend 95% lending without applying punitive Low Equity Margins.

To acquire this miraculous 5% lending facility, you must slip through a narrow eligibility filter:

  • Your gross household income must be strictly under $150,000 (if you are a couple or a solo buyer with dependents), or under $95,000 (if you are a solo buyer without dependents).
  • You must purchase a house under the designated regional price cap (for instance, the cap for a new build in Auckland might be significantly higher than a pre-existing dwelling in Hamilton).
  • The home must be your primary residence—you absolutely cannot rent it out to tenants.

Even better, this 5% deposit does not need to be cold, hard savings. Your KiwiSaver withdrawal, the First Home Grant, and theoretically even a gifted sum from family can legally constitute the entire 5%.

Family Guarantees & Genuine vs. Ungenuine Savings

If your parents own a property with significant freehold equity, they can radically augment your purchasing power through a Family Guarantee (often marketed as 'Springboard' loans). Rather than handing you a briefcase of cash, your parents legally attach a portion of your mortgage to the title of their own house.

For example, if you wish to buy an $800,000 property with zero cash deposit, the bank legally requires $160,000 in equity to satisfy the 20% rule. Your parents can sign a limited guarantee, offering $160,000 of their own home's equity as security. You then borrow the full $800,000 at the absolute best, lowest interest rates without incurring Low Equity Margins. You must still prove your income is high enough to service the massive $800,000 debt, but the entry barrier of capital is bypassed completely.

Finally, a word on "Genuine Savings." If you only possess a 10% deposit, major banks insist that at least 5% of it must be 'genuine.' A cash gift from an aunt, or a sudden inheritance, does not demonstrate financial discipline to a credit assessor. However, long-term KiwiSaver contributions uniformly satisfy the genuine savings requirement. If the entirety of your deposit comprises a cash gift, you may have to hold it in a high-interest savings account for 90 days before the bank deems it legitimate.

Bottom Line

Make no mistake: securing a 20% deposit protects your long-term wealth, limits systemic risk, and extracts the highest possible discount from ruthless bankers. However, waiting 10 years to save that 20% while property values soar is often a numerically inferior strategy compared to stomaching a Low Equity Margin today with a 10% deposit. By strategically combining KiwiSaver, First Home Loans, or Family Guarantees, the "impossible" leap into New Zealand property might be closer than you think.
